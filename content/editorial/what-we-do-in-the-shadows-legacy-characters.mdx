As the final season of FX's critically acclaimed mockumentary, *What We Do in the Shadows*, concluded its run, it marked the end of an era for a show that masterfully blended supernatural absurdity with mundane human-like struggles. Over six seasons, viewers were invited into the dilapidated Staten Island residence of four ancient vampires—Nandor, Laszlo, Nadja, and energy vampire Colin Robinson—alongside Nandor's long-suffering familiar, Guillermo. The series, an offshoot of the 2014 film by Jemaine Clement and Taika Waititi, not only garnered immense critical praise and numerous Emmy nominations but also redefined the comedic potential of the vampire genre, leaving an indelible mark on television and pop culture through its unique characters and sharp writing.

## What happened
*What We Do in the Shadows* premiered on FX in March 2019, quickly establishing itself as a standout in the comedy landscape before concluding its acclaimed run in December 2024. The series, rooted in the same canon as the original New Zealand film and its spin-off *Wellington Paranormal*, chronicles the nightly escapades and domestic squabbles of a quartet of centuries-old vampires living in modern-day Staten Island. The core dynamic revolves around Nandor the Relentless, a 760-year-old former warrior of the Ottoman Empire and the self-proclaimed, albeit ineffectual, leader of the household; Laszlo Cravensworth, a 310-year-old British nobleman with a penchant for the erotic and the scientific; Nadja of Antipaxos, a 500-year-old Greek Romani vampire, Laszlo's wife, and the most overtly aggressive of the group; and Colin Robinson, an energy vampire who feeds on boredom and frustration. Their existence is further complicated by Guillermo de la Cruz, Nandor's loyal human familiar, who dreams of being turned into a vampire himself, a desire constantly at odds with his burgeoning discovery of his ancestry as a descendant of the legendary vampire hunter, Van Helsing.

The show's narrative largely focuses on the vampires' hilarious ineptitude at navigating the complexities of the modern world, their frequent clashes with other supernatural beings—from werewolves to witches—and their own internal power struggles and personal crises. Nandor, despite his formidable past as a bloodthirsty leader, is often depicted as remarkably naive and emotionally stunted, struggling to express genuine affection for Guillermo while constantly frustrating him with his demands and lack of understanding of human customs. Laszlo, turned by Nadja, is portrayed as an eccentric intellectual with varied interests, including scientific experiments on humans, a passion for songwriting (credited with popularizing tunes like "Row, Row, Row Your Boat" and "Come on Eileen"), and an artistic flair for topiary sculptures of vulvas. His pansexuality and preoccupation with sexual thoughts add another layer to his complex, often absurd, character.

Nadja, the most grounded and ambitious of the group, frequently expresses her frustration with her male housemates, often lashing out at Guillermo. Her character is enriched by her centuries-long, perpetually ill-fated affair with a reincarnated knight named Gregor, and her entrepreneurial ventures, including managing her own vampire nightclub. A unique aspect of Nadja's character is the presence of her human ghost, split from her corporeal form when she was turned, which inhabits a doll and offers an additional, often sarcastic, perspective. Guillermo's journey is central to the series, as his decade-plus service to Nandor, fueled by the hope of immortality, slowly gives way to the revelation of his vampire-hunting lineage, creating a profound internal conflict between loyalty and destiny. This intricate web of relationships, combined with the show's distinctive mockumentary style, allowed for a rich tapestry of character-driven comedy and emotional depth, earning *What We Do in the Shadows* widespread critical acclaim, particularly for its exceptional cast and sharp writing, culminating in 35 Emmy Award nominations, including four for Outstanding Comedy Series across its second, third, fifth, and sixth seasons.

## Why it matters
*What We Do in the Shadows* matters because it masterfully subverted and revitalized the vampire genre, transforming traditional horror tropes into a vehicle for profound comedic and character-driven storytelling. By placing ancient, powerful beings in mundane, relatable domestic situations, the show created a unique comedic friction that resonated deeply with audiences. It moved beyond the typical gothic romance or terrifying monster narratives, instead exploring the existential ennui, petty squabbles, and surprising vulnerabilities of immortals grappling with everything from modern technology to local bureaucracy. This approach allowed the series to comment on themes of assimilation, identity, and the absurdity of power dynamics through a supernatural lens, making its fantastical premise feel remarkably grounded and human.

The series also made a significant impact on the mockumentary format itself, demonstrating its versatility beyond workplace comedies. The handheld camera work, direct-to-camera interviews, and the presence of unseen documentarians became integral to the humor, highlighting the vampires' self-importance and their often-delusional perceptions of themselves and the world. This narrative device allowed for intimate character moments and hilarious misunderstandings, pulling viewers into the vampires' bizarre reality while simultaneously maintaining a critical, observational distance. The show’s consistent quality across six seasons, a rarity in modern television, speaks to the strength of its writing and the depth of its character development, ensuring that the humor remained fresh and the relationships continued to evolve, even if at an immortal's pace.

Furthermore, *What We Do in the Shadows* cultivated a passionate fanbase and cemented its place in pop culture through its memorable catchphrases, distinct visual style, and the iconic performances of its ensemble cast. The show's ability to create deeply flawed yet endearing characters, from Nandor's pathetic attempts at leadership to Laszlo's unbridled hedonism and Nadja's fiery ambition, allowed for a wide range of comedic scenarios and emotional beats. Guillermo's journey, in particular, provided a relatable anchor, exploring themes of belonging, self-discovery, and the complex nature of loyalty, resonating with anyone who has felt overlooked or struggled to find their place. Its success has undoubtedly influenced subsequent supernatural comedies and genre-bending shows, proving that even the most ancient myths can find new life and relevance through innovative storytelling and a keen understanding of human (and vampiric) nature.

## How to think about it
To truly appreciate *What We Do in the Shadows*, it's essential to approach it not as a traditional horror series or even a conventional sitcom, but as a character study wrapped in a mockumentary format that explores the absurdity of immortality. The show's brilliance lies in its commitment to the premise: these are ancient, powerful beings who are, at their core, incredibly petty, self-absorbed, and utterly out of touch with the contemporary world. The humor largely stems from this juxtaposition, so embracing the inherent silliness and the vampires' profound lack of self-awareness is key. Don't look for deep philosophical insights into the nature of good and evil; instead, delight in Nandor's naive attempts at romance, Laszlo's bizarre scientific endeavors, Nadja's explosive temper, and Guillermo's perpetually exasperated loyalty. The show is a masterclass in comedic timing and ensemble acting, where much of the humor is derived from the subtle reactions and deadpan deliveries of the cast.

Consider the series as a commentary on the mundane aspects of extraordinary lives. What happens when you've lived for centuries and conquered empires, only to be reduced to arguing over chores or struggling with a faulty internet connection? The show uses its fantastical elements to ground its characters in very human problems, making them relatable despite their monstrous nature. The mockumentary style is not just a gimmick; it's a narrative engine that allows the characters to directly address the audience, offering their often-skewed perspectives and creating an intimate, voyeuristic experience. This format also allows for improvisation and a naturalistic feel, enhancing the comedic impact. Ultimately, *What We Do in the Shadows* invites viewers to suspend disbelief and revel in the sheer joy of watching a dysfunctional family of vampires navigate the bewildering landscape of modern existence, proving that even the undead can be hilariously, tragically, and enduringly human.
