NASA's Plankton, Aerosol, Cloud, and ocean Ecosystem (PACE) satellite, launched with a primary mission to study Earth's oceans and atmosphere, has revealed a powerful, unexpected capability: comprehensive wildfire monitoring. Recent observations showcase PACE's ability to track vast plumes of smoke, identify burn scars, and even assess vegetation health to predict fire risk. This versatility highlights the immense value of multi-purpose space technologies, offering new tools for land managers and scientists grappling with escalating global wildfire challenges. The data gathered provides crucial insights into fire dynamics and their broader environmental impacts.

## What happened
NASA's Plankton, Aerosol, Cloud, and ocean Ecosystem (PACE) satellite, launched in February 2024, was explicitly designed to advance understanding of Earth's oceans and atmosphere. However, it has quickly demonstrated an unexpected and significant capability: observing and monitoring wildfires. Images captured by PACE, such as those showing wildfire smoke over the Great Lakes, illustrate its versatility in tracking large-scale environmental events not directly related to its core oceanographic mission.

The key to this unexpected utility lies in PACE's Ocean Color Instrument (OCI), a hyperspectral imager that observes the planet in hundreds of different wavelengths across the visible, near-infrared, and ultraviolet spectrums. This broad spectral range allows the OCI to gather detailed information beyond its primary ocean color measurements. Scientists can use this data to identify smoke plumes, determine their height in the atmosphere, and differentiate them from other aerosols. Furthermore, the OCI can map burn scars left behind by fires and assess the health of vegetation, including its stress, dryness, and pigment balance, which are crucial indicators for identifying areas at high risk of sparking wildfires.

PACE's ability to view the entire Earth daily, with more frequent coverage at high latitudes, provides a consistent and timely stream of data. This complements existing fire observation instruments and offers a new, comprehensive dataset for scientists. The mission's terrestrial lead, Skye Caplan, noted that "The PACE satellite observes land too, and does it really well," underscoring the richness of this new hyperspectral data for exploring various land-based phenomena, including the precursors and aftermath of wildfires.

## Why it matters
The emergence of PACE's wildfire monitoring capabilities is particularly significant given the increasing frequency and intensity of wildfires globally. As climate patterns shift, understanding fire dynamics, predicting outbreaks, and managing their aftermath becomes more critical. PACE's hyperspectral data offers a new dimension of insight, allowing for more accurate wildfire models and simulations of future events, which can inform policy and mitigation strategies.

For land managers and emergency responders, this data translates into better decision-making. The ability to identify high-risk areas based on vegetation health allows for proactive resource distribution to mitigate fire threats. Post-fire, PACE's rapid assessment of burn scar locations and spans is vital for monitoring areas prone to increased flood and landslide risks, helping communities prepare for secondary hazards. The detailed information on smoke composition and height also aids in understanding air quality impacts and the broader atmospheric transport of pollutants. This unexpected utility transforms PACE from a specialized oceanographic tool into a multi-faceted Earth observatory, providing invaluable intelligence in the face of evolving environmental challenges.

## How to think about it
When considering the implications of PACE's wildfire monitoring capabilities, it's helpful to view modern Earth-observing satellites not just as single-purpose instruments, but as versatile platforms capable of yielding diverse insights. This unexpected utility underscores the value of designing missions with broad observational capabilities, as unforeseen applications can emerge that address critical global challenges. For individuals and communities, this means recognizing that data from seemingly unrelated scientific missions can contribute to local safety and environmental understanding. For policymakers and researchers, it highlights the importance of fostering interdisciplinary collaboration and investing in advanced data analytics to unlock the full potential of space-borne observations. The long-term, consistent data streams from missions like PACE are essential for tracking evolving environmental patterns, informing adaptive strategies, and building resilience against phenomena like wildfires in a changing climate.

<Callout type="warning">
  While PACE provides invaluable data, it's crucial to remember that satellite observations are a component of a larger wildfire management strategy. This data should be integrated with ground-level intelligence, other satellite sources, and aerial surveys for the most comprehensive and actionable insights, rather than being relied upon as a sole source for immediate tactical decisions.
</Callout>
