As the 2026 FIFA World Cup captivated millions across North America, a unique perspective emerged from 250 miles above Earth. Astronauts aboard the International Space Station (ISS) turned their cameras towards the host cities, capturing the sprawling infrastructure and surrounding landscapes of the stadiums where history was being made. These orbital photographs offer more than just a bird's-eye view of sporting venues; they provide a profound look at human civilization's footprint, urban development, and ongoing environmental changes on a planetary scale.

## What happened
Throughout the 2026 FIFA World Cup, which ran from June 11 to July 19 across 16 cities in the United States, Mexico, and Canada, astronauts on the ISS routinely photographed the host venues. Notable captures included the San Francisco Bay Area Stadium (Levi's Stadium) in Santa Clara, California, and the New York New Jersey Stadium (MetLife Stadium) in East Rutherford, New Jersey. These images, taken with high-resolution digital cameras, showcase the intricate details of the stadiums nestled within their urban and natural environments.

The photographs reveal the extensive human impact on these regions. For instance, the San Francisco Bay Area Stadium is situated near the southern part of San Francisco Bay, an area undergoing a 50-year effort to restore up to 90 percent of its salt ponds to tidal wetlands. Similarly, the New York New Jersey Stadium sits within the Meadowlands Sports Complex, an area that has seen centuries of human development, from colonial settlements to industrial uses, now also a site of significant wetland restoration efforts. Other photographed venues include Guadalajara Stadium, Los Angeles Stadium, Houston Stadium, and BC Place Vancouver.

## Why it matters
Observing global events from orbit provides an unparalleled vantage point, transforming a local spectacle into a planetary phenomenon. These images underscore the sheer scale of human endeavor, from constructing massive stadiums to hosting a tournament involving 48 national teams and generating record attendance. Beyond the immediate sporting context, the photographs offer valuable insights into urban planning, infrastructure development, and the long-term environmental consequences and restoration efforts surrounding major metropolitan areas.

The juxtaposition of these modern marvels against the backdrop of ongoing ecological projects highlights humanity's dual impact on Earth—both as builders of complex societies and as stewards (or sometimes disruptors) of natural ecosystems. For scientists, such imagery contributes to long-term datasets tracking land-use change, urban sprawl, and the effectiveness of conservation initiatives, making the World Cup visible not just as a cultural event, but as a data point in our planet's evolving story.
